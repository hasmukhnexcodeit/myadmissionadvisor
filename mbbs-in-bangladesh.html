Bangladesh is the closest international MBBS destination for Indian students — just a 2-hour flight from Kolkata, or even closer by road from border states like West Bengal, Assam, and Tripura. The country shares deep cultural, linguistic and culinary similarities with India, making the transition remarkably smooth for Indian families.

Bangladesh has 35+ NMC-recognised private medical colleges offering MBBS in English medium, with tuition starting at $4,000 per year. Unlike Russia or Central Asia, the proximity to India means students can visit home easily during breaks, and parents can travel to visit without the burden of long-haul travel.

Frequently Asked Questions — MBBS in Bangladesh

Is MBBS from Bangladesh valid in India? +
Yes. All MCI/NMC-listed Bangladeshi medical universities grant MBBS degrees that are fully valid in India. Graduates must clear the FMGE/NEXT exam before practicing, the same requirement as any other foreign MBBS degree.
What is the medium of teaching in Bangladesh medical colleges? +
Teaching is 100% in English, with curricula specifically designed for foreign students, including a large cohort from India, Nepal, and other South Asian countries.
How long does the Bangladesh student visa take? +
Typically 7–10 working days, making it one of the fastest visa processes among all MBBS abroad destinations. MyAdmissionAdvisor handles the complete documentation process for you.
Is the food in Bangladesh suitable for Indian students? +
Yes, very much so. Bangladeshi cuisine closely mirrors Bengali Indian food — rice, dal, fish curry and vegetables are dietary staples. Vegetarian options are also readily available on request in most hostels.
What is the total cost of MBBS in Bangladesh? +
Total 5-year cost (tuition + hostel + food) typically ranges from ₹22 to ₹38 lakhs depending on the college, making it slightly higher than Russia or Kyrgyzstan but offset by lower travel costs and proximity to home.
Can I travel home frequently while studying in Bangladesh? +
Yes. Being just a 2-hour flight (or a road journey from border states) from major Indian cities, many students travel home during festivals and semester breaks — something not feasible for students in Russia or Central Asia.
